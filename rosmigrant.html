Migrant

Mobile · B2C · Government compliance · 800K MAU

Selected achievements

  • Identified why 60% of users abandoned the core flow through user research.
  • Redesigned the check experience around education: completions +30%, task time −40%, premium conversion +4% (A/B tested).
  • Unified different check flows into one consistent pattern.

RosMigrant helps migrants from Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan, Tajikistan, and Kyrgyzstan track their legal status in Russia. Missing a government check can mean fines, deportation, or losing a job. 800,000 people used the app every month. A large share of them opened it to complete a check and left without finishing.

My role: Product Designer. I owned the document input flow, worked on subscription UX, ran user research, and co-designed the A/B test for the new subscription logic with the PM.

The problem

60% of users dropped off on the document input screen.

I interviewed 20 users and conducted usability classes, watching them fill out forms. The same thing happened: people picked up their documents, looked at the column labeled "migration card series" and did not understand what to do. Some people didn't trust the app enough to enter their personal information. The average shutdown time was about 5 minutes, as users were stuck.

The constraints

We looked at two options: full redesign including navigation and gamification, or targeted fixes to the existing flow.

Full redesign was off the table — too risky for a live product with 800K users. So we focused on the specific moments where users lost the thread.

What I did

Embedded explanations in the form itself. The problem was that the users had no idea why they were filling them out. We have implemented short training videos directly into the verification process: which document to open, which number to find, and why this verification is important. My job was to work out where and how this content is displayed — how it relates to the state of the form when it launches, how it doesn't interfere with users who don't need it.

Made every check type feel the same. Different checks had different flows — different entry points, different field orders. A user who'd done one type had no idea how to do the next. One pattern instead of five.

Cut the redundant steps. A flow audit turned up screens that existed for technical reasons but confused users — repeated confirmations, unclear intermediate states. I removed what could be removed and made the remaining steps feel like a sequence, not a maze.

Reframed the subscription. Premium plans weren't converting because users didn't connect the features to their own situation. I redesigned the presentation around the user's actual workflow. We tested the new logic on a user segment over 3 weeks.

What I learned

The users I was designing for came from places where government processes are opaque and getting them wrong has real consequences. Their distrust wasn't irrational.

The most effective thing we did wasn't simplifying the form. It was explaining why it existed. Education inside the flow worked better than any structural change because it caught users at the moment of confusion — not before it, in an onboarding screen they'd already skipped.

If I were starting over, I'd push for the instructional content earlier. The research was pointing at a trust problem from day one. We spent weeks on structural hypotheses before getting there.
